01

How software teams work

Why finance and tech feel like different planets

In audit and FP&A, work is structured around deliverables: a report, a model, a filing. The scope is agreed upfront. The timeline is set. You know what "done" looks like before you start. This is sometimes called the waterfall model — each phase flows into the next, and you do not go back.

Technology teams discovered a problem with this: by the time you deliver the final product, the business has changed its mind. Or worse — building it revealed that the original idea was wrong. Six months of work, delivered too late to matter.

The solution was to stop waiting until everything was perfect, and instead deliver something small every two weeks. Get feedback. Adjust. Repeat. This is the foundation of Agile.

Waterfall vs Agile — how work is delivered differently

The sprint in practice

A sprint is a fixed two-week window. At the start — a sprint planning meeting — the team decides what gets built. At the end — a sprint review — they demonstrate it to stakeholders. Then the cycle repeats.

As an analyst, your work will be broken into sprint-sized chunks. "Is that in scope for this sprint?" is not a bureaucratic question — it is asking whether the work has been planned and resourced for the current two weeks.

The daily standup

Every morning the team gathers for 15 minutes. The standup has a strict format because its purpose is narrow: surface blockers fast, keep everyone aligned. It is not a meeting. It is a pulse check.

What happens in a standup — and what does not

Tickets and Jira

Every piece of work on a data or engineering team lives as a ticket in a tool called Jira. Think of it as a task manager where the whole team can see the status of every piece of work at once.

A ticket has: a title, a description, an assignee, a priority, and a status. The status moves through a fixed set of columns — To Do, In Progress, In Review, Done. Your job is to keep your tickets updated. If you finished a task yesterday but your ticket still says "In Progress," the PM sees a team with unresolved work. Your standup says one thing; Jira says another. That gap erodes trust.

Practical rule: Before your morning standup, open Jira and move any tickets that changed status overnight. Make your standup answer match your board — every time.

Product Owner vs Project Manager

These two roles are easy to confuse because both sit near the top of the team structure and both attend the same meetings. The difference is in what they own.

Product Owner

Owns: the backlog — the prioritised list of everything that needs to be built.

Answers: What should we build? Why? In what order?

Go to them when: a requirement seems wrong, a scope question needs resolving, or you are not sure if something is worth building at all.

They represent the business. Their job is to make sure the team builds the right thing.

Project Manager

Owns: the timeline, the dependencies, the process.

Answers: Is the team on track? What is blocked? When will it be done?

Go to them when: you are blocked and need someone to unblock you, a deadline is at risk, or you need clarity on process.

They represent the schedule. Their job is to make sure the right thing gets built on time.

Simple test: "What and why?" → Product Owner. "How and when?" → Project Manager. On smaller teams one person sometimes fills both roles — ask which hat they are wearing when you approach them.
02

Dev, UAT, and prod

Imagine you are working on a live dashboard that 50 people at a bank check every morning. You make a quick change to a measure. The change introduces a bug. Now all 50 people are looking at wrong numbers — and nobody knows yet.

This is exactly why software teams never work directly in the system real users see. Instead, there are three separate environments, each with a distinct purpose:

The three environments — how code moves from idea to production

What this looks like in Power BI

In the Power BI service, workspaces map to environments. A client engagement typically has three workspaces:

  • [Client Name] - Development — where you build reports and models
  • [Client Name] - UAT — where the client reviews your work before it goes live
  • [Client Name] - Production — what end users open every morning

The access you are granted tells you which environment you are in. If you have access to all three, be deliberate about which workspace you are publishing to. One wrong publish to the wrong workspace is enough to cause an incident.

The .pbip file format (Power BI Projects) stores your report as folders of JSON and text files — this is what enables version control. You do not need to read or edit these files. You do need to know they exist, because when a colleague mentions "committing to the repo" or "the pipeline deployed," this is what they are talking about.

03

Access & permissions

On the finance side, getting access to a report usually meant asking a colleague and receiving an email with the file attached. In a technology environment at a regulated institution, this is not how it works — and the reason matters.

Financial data is subject to regulatory oversight. Every access grant creates an entry in an audit log. If something goes wrong — a data breach, an incorrect regulatory submission — the audit trail of who had access to what is how the investigation begins. The formal process is not bureaucracy for its own sake. It is the paper trail that protects the institution, and incidentally protects you.

How access requests flow — what happens after you submit

Principle of least privilege

This is a security concept that sounds formal but is intuitive once you understand it: request only the access you need to do your current job, nothing more. If you need to read a report, request read access. If you need to publish reports, request contributor access. Do not request owner or admin access "just in case."

The reason is not about trust. It is about containing the blast radius of mistakes. If your credentials are compromised, or if you accidentally delete something, the damage is limited to what you had access to. More access means more exposure.

How to write a good access request: State the specific resource, the access level needed, and why. "I need read access to the [Client Name] - Development Power BI workspace in order to build the LCR dashboard in the current sprint." That is a good request. "I need Power BI access" is not.
04

Tools & day-one setup

The first day at a new client engagement is chaotic. Laptops are being provisioned, logins are failing, access requests are pending. The single thing you can control in advance is your phone. Set it up before you need it — because the one time you cannot receive an authentication code is the one time your manager needs you on a call immediately.

Phone apps to install before day one

  • Microsoft Authenticator — required for multi-factor authentication on every Microsoft login. Without it, you cannot log in to anything. Install first.
  • Microsoft Teams — your primary channel for client calls, messages, and file sharing. Enable notifications. You will be expected to respond within a couple of hours during business hours.
  • Outlook mobile — email on the go. Clients send time-sensitive requests by email. Checking twice a day is not enough.
  • Your organisation's VPN app — your IT team will specify which one. Some client systems are only reachable over VPN.
  • Slack — some clients and internal teams use this alongside or instead of Teams. Ask on day one.

Virtual machines — what they are and why they exist

A virtual machine (VM) is a full computer that runs inside your computer. It has its own operating system, its own software, and its own connection to a network — the client's network. When you log into a client VM, you are effectively using a computer that lives inside the client's infrastructure.

Why do clients use them? Control and security. The client decides what software is installed. They can monitor activity. If you leave the engagement, they revoke access and the VM is gone — your work stays inside their system. Your local machine never had any of it.

Inside the VM — client world

Client data, tools, and reports live here.

You work with these during the engagement.

Nothing leaves without going through their security controls.

Your local machine — your world

Your personal files, notes, and non-client work.

You cannot copy client data here.

Treat the two as completely separate environments.

The VM will feel slow. This is normal — you are accessing a computer over a network. Do not try to fix it by installing software. If it is genuinely unusable, raise it with IT through the proper channel.
05

Compliance at a financial institution

Working as a consultant inside a financial institution means operating under compliance frameworks that most industries never encounter. None of this is optional. None of it is negotiable. And none of it should come as a surprise if you understand the stakes.

Holdings disclosure

Financial institutions are required by regulation to manage conflicts of interest. If you are working on systems that touch trading data, investment positions, or liquidity metrics, the institution needs to know whether you personally hold securities that could create a conflict.

When you begin an engagement, you will be asked to disclose your personal investment holdings — stocks, bonds, funds, crypto, anything significant. This is standard. Every contractor goes through it. Complete it accurately, and complete it on time.

If you are unsure whether something needs to be disclosed — disclose it. The risk of disclosing something that turned out to be unnecessary is exactly zero. The risk of failing to disclose something you should have is a compliance violation with real consequences. When in doubt, include it.

Annual certifications

You will be enrolled in recurring online certification modules. Typical modules include: information security awareness, anti-money laundering (AML) basics, data classification and handling, conflicts of interest, and physical security. Each is usually 20–40 minutes with a short quiz at the end.

These are not optional. Missing a certification deadline can result in your access to client systems being suspended until the certification is completed. Track your deadlines. Treat them like client deliverables.

Data handling — the non-negotiable rules

  • Client data does not leave the client environment — not by email, not by USB, not by screenshot to a personal device
  • Do not save client files to personal cloud storage: Google Drive, Dropbox, iCloud, personal OneDrive
  • Do not photograph your screen when it shows client data
  • Do not discuss specific client data, positions, or findings in personal channels or with people outside the engagement
  • If you are unsure whether something counts as sensitive — assume it does and ask before acting
06

Corporate communication

Communication in a consulting environment operates on a set of unstated conventions that finance professionals do not always encounter. The stakes are higher than they might seem — how you communicate directly affects how competent and reliable you appear to clients and colleagues who have not yet seen your work.

Choosing the right channel

One of the most common mistakes is treating all communication as equivalent — firing off a Teams message for something that needs an email, or scheduling a meeting for something that could be a screenshot. The right channel depends on what the communication is for.

Which channel to use — a decision framework

Writing effective emails

Email is for things that need a record, require a formal response, or involve people outside your immediate team. The subject line is not decoration — it is the first piece of information the recipient uses to decide when and how to respond.

Subject line — good vs bad

✗  Question — tells the reader nothing, creates anxiety

✗  Re: Re: Re: LCR update — buried in a thread, no action visible

✓  LCR inflow table — UAT sign-off needed by Thursday — what it is about, what is needed, when

✓  2052A paginated report — ready for your review in UAT — clear, specific, no action ambiguity

One ask per email. If you have three questions, send one email with three numbered questions — not three separate emails. Reply-all only when every person on the thread genuinely needs your response.

Writing effective chat messages

The single most important rule in chat: state what you need in the first message. "Hi, do you have a minute?" forces the other person to stop what they are doing, respond, wait for your actual question, and then decide whether they can help. That is three context switches before any value is exchanged.

Teams / Slack — before and after

✗  "Hi, do you have a minute?"

✗  "Hey, quick question about the dashboard"

✓  "Hey — the LCR outflow measure is showing a different total than UAT. I think it is a date filter issue. I tried removing REMOVEFILTERS from the date column but it did not help. Can you take a look when you have a moment?"

Corporate idioms — what people actually mean

These phrases are used constantly. They are not jargon for the sake of it — each one packs a specific meaning into a short phrase. Not knowing them makes meetings confusing.

"Let's take this offline"
This topic needs more than the current meeting can handle — let's discuss separately with only the relevant people.
"Circle back"
Follow up on this later — either with more information or after checking with someone else.
"Socialise the idea"
Share informally with key stakeholders before the formal proposal, so there are no surprises in the meeting.
"Hard stop at [time]"
I have another commitment starting at that time and must leave this call punctually — do not run over.
"In the weeds"
Lost in detail, losing sight of the bigger picture or the goal of the conversation.
"Level set"
Make sure everyone in the room has the same baseline understanding before proceeding.
"Boil the ocean"
Trying to solve too much at once — scope is too broad, focus on what matters now.
"Action item"
A specific task with a clear owner and a deadline — not a vague intention, but a commitment.
07

Working as a consultant

The one-on-one

A one-on-one (1:1) is a regular meeting between you and your direct manager — usually 30 minutes, weekly or biweekly. The key thing to understand: this is your time, not your manager's. It is not a status report. It is a dedicated space for you to raise things you cannot raise in a standup or Slack.

Come prepared. Bring a short list: what you have been working on since last time, any blockers you need help removing, questions about priorities or direction, and anything you are confused or uncertain about. Managers who have prepared employees leave 1:1s more informed and more confident in the person they are managing.

When to ask for help — the 20-minute rule

There is a real tension between two legitimate goals: developing independence by working through problems yourself, and not wasting time going down the wrong path when someone else could point you in the right direction in two minutes.

A practical rule that works: spend 20 minutes genuinely trying to solve the problem yourself. After 20 minutes without meaningful progress, ask for help. But when you ask, come with context — what you tried, what you expected, what actually happened. This accomplishes two things: it shows you did the work, and it dramatically speeds up the help you receive.

Asking for help — what good looks like

✗  "My measure is broken, can you help?"

✓  "I've been working on the LCR net outflow measure for 25 minutes. I tried wrapping it in CALCULATE with REMOVEFILTERS on the date table but the total still changes when I slice by entity. I expected the entity filter not to affect it. Can you take a look?"

Screenshots as the default communication

In finance, showing someone your work usually meant scheduling a meeting. In a consulting environment, this is unnecessarily expensive — a meeting takes 30 minutes of two people's time, requires scheduling, and creates no record. A screenshot with a one-line explanation takes 30 seconds and can be acted on asynchronously.

Make this a habit: before you schedule a meeting to show something, ask whether a screenshot would do the job. Often it will. The meetings you do have will be more productive because they are reserved for things that genuinely need real-time discussion.

Working with a data engineer

Data engineers build and maintain the pipelines that move data from source systems — in your case, systems like Axiom — to the places where you access it. Your relationship with them affects how smoothly the project runs.

What they need from you: specific requirements, in writing, with enough lead time. "I need the LCR data" is not a requirement. "I need a fact table with inflow and outflow amounts by product type and counterparty, at daily granularity, filtered to the past 90 days" is a requirement. The more specific you are, the faster they can deliver what you actually need.

What to never do: modify a table or pipeline they own without telling them. Even if you have the access to do it, making changes to shared infrastructure without communication is the fastest way to break trust with a data engineering team.

The five questions before opening Power BI

The most common mistake analysts make at the start of a project is opening Power BI before they understand what they are building. The result is a dashboard that technically works but does not answer the question the stakeholder actually had — and now you are rebuilding it.

Before you open a single file, get clear answers to these five questions:

1
Who will use this, and how often?

The answer changes everything — a daily dashboard for a liquidity risk officer looks very different from a monthly summary for senior management.

2
What decision does this help them make?

If you cannot answer this, the stakeholder probably cannot either — and that is the first thing to resolve. No decision = no purpose = no scope.

3
What is the most important number on this report?

There is always one. Finding it tells you what the model must calculate correctly above all else, and what to validate first.

4
Where does the data come from, and who owns it?

You need to know who to talk to when the data is wrong, incomplete, or structured differently than expected — and it will be.

5
What does "correct" look like — is there an existing report or spreadsheet to match?

If yes, that spreadsheet is your acceptance test. If no, you need to define acceptance criteria before you start building.

08

Regulatory context — Axiom, LCR, and 2052A

What Axiom is

Axiom is a regulatory data management platform used by financial institutions to collect, validate, and submit reports to regulators including the Federal Reserve, the OCC, and equivalent bodies in other jurisdictions. It is the system of record for regulatory submissions.

What makes Axiom data unusual is that its structure is defined by the regulator, not the bank. The column names, the row hierarchies, the codes — these come from the regulatory form template, not from a business data model. When you first look at an Axiom dataset, it will not look like a clean, intuitive data source. That is expected. The structure is optimised for regulatory accuracy, not analytical convenience.

LCR — what it measures and why it matters

The Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) is a regulatory requirement that measures whether a bank holds enough high-quality liquid assets (HQLA) to survive a 30-day stress scenario — the kind of scenario where funding markets seize up and depositors withdraw money simultaneously. The ratio must be at or above 100%.

LCR formula

LCR = High-Quality Liquid Assets ÷ Net Cash Outflows (30-day stress) × 100

Must be ≥ 100% at all times. Breaching 100% triggers regulatory escalation.

The LCR is calculated and reported daily by large institutions. The dashboard you will work with in the capstone visualises this calculation — which means every number on it has a direct line to a regulatory submission.

The 2052A report

The 2052A is the Federal Reserve's daily liquidity monitoring report, submitted by systemically important financial institutions (SIFIs). It captures inflows, outflows, and collateral at a very granular level — the same data that feeds the LCR calculation, but in a format the Fed can aggregate across institutions.

When you are working with LCR data from Axiom, you are working with data that has been shaped to meet 2052A specifications. This is why the column names look like regulatory codes, why some rows represent categories defined by the Fed, and why the granularity is finer than you would design if you were building a data model from scratch.

Why accuracy matters more here than almost anywhere else: A 1% error in a management dashboard is a conversation. A 1% error in a regulatory submission is a formal inquiry, a remediation process, and potentially a public disclosure event. The bar is different. Validate everything.
